## The Political Landscape

Mayor Brandon Johnson governs a city where the council majority has slipped from his grasp and federal policy collides daily with municipal authority. The former Chicago Teachers Union organizer won office on a progressive platform, but by early 2026 his agenda runs headlong into a council bloc that rejected his signature corporate head tax and is now dictating its own revenue plan. A working group led by Aldermen Scott Waguespack (32nd), Gilbert Villegas (36th), and Samantha Nugent (39th) is generating alternatives — advertising on city assets, a cloud computing tax, a plastic bag tax, selling old debt — while the mayor warns of a mid-year shortfall ([City Council majority turns up the heat on Mayor Brandon Johnson to implement alternative budget](https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2026/02/11/chicago-city-council-majority-turns-up-the-heat-on-mayor-brandon-johnson-to-implement-alternative-budget)).

The clearest fault line in the council is fiscal. Johnson's 2026 budget assumed $6.8 million from newly legalized video gambling terminals, but his own administration is quietly working to repeal that provision. Ald. Anthony Beale (9th) went public with the accusation and rallied at least 15 council members to force the Illinois Gaming Board to start accepting license applications ([Ald. Beale says Johnson team is working behind the scenes to repeal video gambling](https://chicago.suntimes.com/fran-spielman-show/2026/02/06/ald-anthony-beale-video-gambling-possible-repeal-brandon-johnson-walter-red-burnett)). The mayor then vetoed a council-passed hemp ban, and the override attempt fell eight votes short — a rare Johnson win that exposed different factional lines on public health versus small-business interests ([Hemp ban dies after City Council upholds Mayor Johnson's veto](https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2026/02/18/hemp-ban-brandon-johnson-veto-override-attempt-defeated-chicago-city-council)).

A second major fault line is between City Hall and Cook County's independent prosecutors. Johnson signed an executive order directing Chicago police to gather evidence of alleged ICE abuse and refer cases to the Cook County State's Attorney. Eileen O'Neill Burke called the order "wholly inappropriate" and warned it could jeopardize criminal prosecutions ([County's top prosecutor calls Mayor Brandon Johnson's ICE executive order 'wholly inappropriate'](https://chicago.suntimes.com/immigration/2026/02/06/eileen-oneill-burke-brandon-johnson-immigration-ice-law)). Meanwhile, Cook County Board President Toni Preckwinkle and a majority of county commissioners pressured Burke from the other direction, demanding she investigate and charge federal agents involved in two Chicago-area shootings ([Preckwinkle calls on top Cook County prosecutor to investigate ICE](https://chicago.suntimes.com/immigration/2026/02/12/preckwinkle-calls-on-top-cook-county-prosecutor-to-investigate-ice-feds-involved-in-chicago-area-shootings)). ## Major Issues and Ongoing Battles

### 1. The Budget War

The 2026 budget is not settled — it's a rolling fight. The council majority rejected Johnson's corporate head tax and is now assembling its own revenue package. Waguespack, Villegas, and Nugent are leading working groups on advertising revenue, debt sales, and new taxes while the mayor warns of a possible mid-year shortfall ([City Council majority turns up the heat on Mayor Brandon Johnson to implement alternative budget](https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2026/02/11/chicago-city-council-majority-turns-up-the-heat-on-mayor-brandon-johnson-to-implement-alternative-budget)). The Civic Federation has called for furloughs and cuts; the Chicago Federation of Labor's Bob Reiter says unions have offered efficiency measures instead ([Civic Federation scapegoats city workers](https://chicago.suntimes.com/letters-to-the-editor/2026/02/07/civic-federation-city-workers-epstein-school-vouchers-kennedy-center-disabled-taxes-cps-protests)).

The administration already has a cash-flow problem. A $260 million advance pension payment was split because delayed Cook County property tax receipts left the city short. Officials insist a $1 billion TIF surplus earmarked for CPS won't jeopardize the bond rating, but the explanation strains credibility ([Mayoral aides explain Chicago's cash flow crunch that triggered partial pension payment](https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2026/02/09/cash-flow-crunch-partial-pension-payment-mayor-brandon-johnson)). On the revenue side, the video gambling fight is a proxy war: Beale and allies want the projected $6.8 million; the mayor's camp cites gambling-addiction concerns and the risk of undercutting Bally's casino ([Ald. Beale is convinced Johnson is buying time to repeal video gambling](https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2026/02/10/ald-anthony-beale-video-gambling-ban-lift-mayor-brandon-johnson-repeal)).

Business license fees jumped by as much as 400% in 2026 with little advance notice, hitting small businesses hard ([Business license fees increased this year, some by as high as 400%](https://chicago.suntimes.com/small-business/2026/02/23/business-license-fees-increased-chicago)). And a circuit judge ruled the city overcharged motorists on city-sticker and parking violations, potentially creating $104 million in refunds plus $34 million in interest — a bill nobody budgeted for ([$1,600 in fines for 4 city sticker violations? City must refund millions for ticket overcharges](https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2026/02/24/chicago-overcharge-city-sticker-violations-parking-tickets-fine-cap-cook-county-ruling-refunds)).

**Unresolved:** Will the council's alternative revenue plan produce enough to close the gap? What happens if video gambling is repealed and the $6.8 million vanishes? How does the city absorb $100 million-plus in sticker-fine refunds?

### 2. Immigration Enforcement and the ICE Standoff

Federal immigration operations — particularly "Operation Midway Blitz" — have rattled neighborhoods like Brighton Park, Little Village, and Pilsen ([Voters cast ballots at Loop Supersite as early voting kicks off](https://chicago.suntimes.com/elections/2026/02/12/voters-cast-ballots-at-loop-supersite-as-early-voting-kicks-off-everythings-at-stake); [Chicago after the blitz](https://chicago.suntimes.com/morning-edition/2026/02/23/operation-midway-blitz-trump-jesse-jackson-archdiocese-of-chicago)). Two Chicago-area shootings — the fatal shooting of Silverio Villegas González in Franklin Park and the wounding of Marimar Martínez — have become flashpoints ([Preckwinkle calls on top Cook County prosecutor to investigate ICE](https://chicago.suntimes.com/immigration/2026/02/12/preckwinkle-calls-on-top-cook-county-prosecutor-to-investigate-ice-feds-involved-in-chicago-area-shootings)).

Multiple officials are pressing for accountability from different angles. Johnson's executive order directing CPD to investigate ICE abuse drew Burke's rebuke. Cook County commissioners pressured Burke to charge the agents. Burke and Attorney General Kwame Raoul then announced a prosecution protocol. Separately, attorney Sheryl Weikal petitioned for a special prosecutor to handle ICE cases ([How can Chicago's top prosecutor hold ICE accountable?](https://chicago.suntimes.com/public-safety/2026/02/06/how-can-chicagos-top-prosecutor-hold-ice-accountable)). Prosecutors also dropped charges against 19 of 21 people arrested at a clergy-led protest at the Broadview ICE facility ([Cook County prosecutors drop charges against 19 arrested during clergy-led protest at Broadview ICE facility](https://chicago.suntimes.com/immigration/2026/02/09/cook-county-prosecutors-drop-charges-against-19-arrested-during-clergy-led-protest-broadview-ice-facility)).

The economic ripple effects are tangible. A "buy local" campaign was organized in Brighton Park after raids hammered Latino-owned businesses, with microgrants of $1,500 to $5,000 distributed ([Campaña de compra local impulsa Brighton Park](https://chicago.suntimes.com/la-voz/2026/02/17/campana-de-compra-local-impulsa-brighton-park-tras-redadas-de-inmigracion-que-perjudicaron-a-negocios-locales)).

**Unresolved:** Can the Burke-Raoul protocol actually result in criminal charges against federal agents, or is it symbolic? Does Johnson's executive order survive legal challenge? What accountability mechanism, if any, will stick?

### 4. Chicago Public Schools: Money, Leadership, and Control

CPS is in a three-front crisis: a $500 million deficit, a stalled superintendent search, and a power struggle over who controls the schools. The board is divided over the search for a permanent superintendent, with the board president saying it's on track while six elected members say mayoral interference has stalled it ([Is CPS superintendent search 'on track' or 'stalled'?](https://chicago.suntimes.com/chicago-school-board/2026/02/13/is-cps-superintendent-search-on-track-or-stalled-depends-who-you-ask-on-school-board)). The board will become fully elected later this year, and big money is already flowing into November races — billionaire Michael Sacks and the SEIU Illinois Council PAC have made record donations ([Big money expected to flow in November's historic Chicago school board race](https://chicago.suntimes.com/chicago-school-board/2026/02/19/big-money-expected-to-flow-in-novembers-historic-chicago-school-board-race)).

The Aspira charter network, serving about 570 high school students, is insolvent and facing closure. Most board members oppose a bailout, and CPS will scatter students to new schools ([CPS to shut down financially distressed ASPIRA campuses](https://chicago.suntimes.com/education/2026/02/26/cps-to-shut-down-financially-distressed-aspira-campuses-scattering-students-to-new-schools)). CPS is also eyeing changes to the Chicago High School for the Arts that would cut conservatory instruction time, angering parents ([CPS eyes changes to ChiArts conservatory model](https://chicago.suntimes.com/education/2026/02/25/cps-eyes-changes-to-chiarts-conservatory-model-upsetting-parents-who-want-more-arts-during-school-day)). A slavery reenactment incident at Portage Park Elementary prompted district-wide scrutiny of how sensitive historical topics are taught ([CPS mother outraged by history lesson her child says included reenactment of slavery](https://chicago.suntimes.com/2026/02/10/cps-mother-outraged-by-history-lesson-her-child-says-included-reenactment-of-slavery)).

**Unresolved:** Can CPS close its deficit without the borrowing the board says it can't do? Who will become the permanent superintendent, and will the elected board or the mayor choose? What happens to Aspira's 545 displaced students?

## Follow the Money

**The pension cash-flow problem.** The city split a $260 million advance pension payment because Cook County property tax receipts arrived late. The administration blamed Tyler Technologies' system overhaul and other outflows, but the split raises questions about structural liquidity. A $1 billion TIF surplus was earmarked for CPS. Officials say the bond rating is safe; rating agencies haven't weighed in publicly. ([Mayoral aides explain Chicago's cash flow crunch](https://chicago.suntimes.com/city-hall/2026/02/09/cash-flow-crunch-partial-pension-payment-mayor-brandon-johnson))

**Police misconduct settlements.** The city faces $29.1 million in pending Guevara settlements alone. The Tate family won $5.7 million at trial. The Finance Committee rejected an $8.25 million settlement for the family of a woman killed in a police chase — which doesn't make the liability go away. Track the total annual payout from police litigation; it's a recurring multi-hundred-million-dollar drag on the budget.
