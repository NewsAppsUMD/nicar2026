## County Overview & Context

**County Overview & Context – Talbot County (2025)**  

Talbot County’s public‑school system serves 4,529 K‑12 students in a county of 37,663 residents, with a school‑age population of 5,280. The district is 68 % White, 15 % Black, 16 % Hispanic/Latino and under 1 % Asian, and 9.3 % of students live in poverty. Median household income ($81,667) and broadband access (88.1 %) rank above the state average, yet achievement gaps persist across race and socioeconomic lines.  

Academic performance is uneven. Chapel District Elementary leads the district with 5th‑grade proficiency well above state benchmarks in ELA (67.5 % + 22.5 pts), Math (55.0 % + 24.3 pts) and Science (45.0 % + 19.4 pts). By contrast, Easton Elementary falls far behind, posting 5th‑grade scores of 19.8 % ELA (‑25.2 pts), 11.9 % Math (‑18.8 pts) and 11.9 % Science (‑13.7 pts). Middle‑school data show a similar split: Easton Middle’s 8th‑grade Math proficiency is a stark 5.2 % (‑3.5 pts), while St. Michael’s Middle/High reaches 49.2 % in ELA (just above state). High‑school results are mixed; St. Michael’s High School posts strong ELA (76.7 % + 17.2 pts) but algebra remains at 7.2 % (‑14.2 pts), and Easton High lags in both ELA (56.6 % ‑2.9 pts) and Math (5.0 % ‑16.4 pts). Science scores hover around state average, with a modest 5‑point gain for the county in 2024‑25 MCAP results [8].

Discipline patterns echo the achievement divide. Easton High recorded 170 suspensions in the 2023‑2024 school year, the highest in the district, while Easton Middle logged 94. In contrast, Chapel District and White Marsh elementary schools reported fewer than ten suspensions each. The Board of Education has responded by commissioning a district‑wide discipline audit that will examine racial and socioeconomic disparities and align policies with the Blueprint for Maryland’s Future [2].  

Governance is anchored by Superintendent Dr. Sharon Pepukayi and a five‑member board led by President Emily Jackson. The board’s recent actions illustrate a dual focus on facilities and equity. In October 2025, the board approved a $1.2 million renovation of the East High School auditorium and a $450,000 window‑replacement project at Chapel Elementary, leveraging a Maryland Center for School Safety grant [1]. The same month, the board allocated $150,000 for a summer tutoring program and partnered with the state Department of Education for additional analytics to address test‑score stagnation [5]. Board members have also been active in regional policy discussions, meeting with Maryland Association of Counties leaders to coordinate Blueprint funding strategies ahead of the 2026 General Assembly [4].

The district’s challenges are compounded by statewide trends. Maryland’s latest data show a reversal in girls’ STEM gains, with female enrollment in advanced math and science courses dropping 5 % in 2024‑25 [6]; Talbot County mirrors this decline, prompting the state to earmark $500,000 for targeted outreach [6]. Meanwhile, the Mid‑Shore region’s mixed MCAP outcomes—Talbot’s modest science gain offset by persistently low math scores—highlight the urgency of Blueprint‑driven interventions [8].

## Top Three Issues on the Education Beat

### Academic Achievement Gaps and the Erosion of STEM Gains  

The 2024‑25 MCAP data reveal a district that is struggling to keep pace with state benchmarks. Talbot County’s overall proficiency slipped to 45.4 %—a 1.9‑point decline from the previous year [9], while individual elementary schools paint a stark contrast. Chapel District Elementary posted a 67.5 % proficiency rate in Grade‑5 ELA and 55 % in Math, each well above state averages [1], whereas Easton Elementary fell to single‑digit percentages in all three subjects, with Math languishing at 11.9 % [1]. The disparity between the district’s highest‑performing and lowest‑performing schools underscores an achievement gap that aligns closely with socioeconomic and racial demographics: the district’s Black and Hispanic populations together account for roughly 31 % of enrollment, yet many of the lower‑scoring schools serve a higher concentration of these students [Demographics].

Compounding the achievement issue, recent coverage highlights a reversal in the district’s earlier gains in female participation in advanced STEM courses. After a modest 12 % rise in girls’ enrollment in rigorous math and science classes during 2022‑23, the 2024‑25 school year saw a 5 % drop, leaving females at just 38 % of all STEM seats [6]. State‑wide data confirm a similar trend, with girls’ math scores falling three points and science scores two points relative to boys [7]. District leaders have earmarked $500,000 for outreach and mentorship programs, but the funding arrives against a backdrop of budget constraints and a shortage of qualified STEM teachers, as reported by school officials [6].

Board discussions in September 2025 emphasized that the achievement shortfall is not merely a test‑score problem but a symptom of uneven curricular support and limited after‑school resources. The Board voted to hire 12 additional literacy coaches and expand tutoring, yet those measures are being rolled out in a district where only 88 % of households have broadband access [Demographics], a factor that hampers equitable participation in online supplemental instruction. Until the district can close the resource gap between its high‑performing and under‑performing schools, the overall proficiency trend is likely to continue its downward drift.

### Aging Infrastructure and School Safety Investments  

The Talbot County Board of Education has been actively pursuing capital projects aimed at modernizing facilities and meeting heightened safety standards. In October 2025 the Board approved a $1.2 million contract to upgrade the East High School auditorium and a $450 000 contract to replace windows at Chapel District Elementary, both funded through a Maryland Center for School Safety grant [1]. The same funding stream supports a broader $6.2 million renovation of Chapel that includes a new gym, upgraded HVAC, and classroom modernization, with construction slated to finish by spring 2026 [10]. These projects reflect an urgent response to aging building stock that, in past audits, failed to meet contemporary energy‑efficiency and security benchmarks.

Safety drills have run in parallel with the capital agenda. In July 2025 the district coordinated active‑shooter simulations involving law‑enforcement and EMS agencies across Talbot, Caroline, and Dorchester counties [13][14]. The exercises tested lockdown protocols, communication chains, and medical triage, producing after‑action reports that will inform future emergency‑response plans. While the drills demonstrate a proactive stance on school safety, they also reveal the strain on limited resources: coordinating multi‑jurisdictional responses requires both personnel time and financial outlay that compete with other budget priorities, such as instructional staffing and technology upgrades.

Board members have repeatedly framed these infrastructure and safety initiatives within the larger context of the Blueprint for Maryland’s Future, which mandates modern, secure learning environments as a prerequisite for academic improvement. However, the reliance on external grant funding and the staggered timeline of construction mean that many schools will continue to operate in sub‑optimal conditions for the near term. The district’s challenge, therefore, is to balance the immediate need for safe, functional spaces with the long‑term fiscal sustainability of ongoing capital projects.

### Discipline Equity and Growing Student Homelessness  

Disciplinary data from the 2023‑2024 school year expose a stark contrast within the district: Chapel District Elementary recorded only eight suspensions, while Easton Elementary logged forty [1]. The Board’s October 2025 directive to examine discipline trends across six Eastern Shore counties signals concern that punitive measures may be unevenly applied, potentially reflecting racial or socioeconomic bias [2]. Superintendent Pepukayi emphasized the need for data‑driven, equity‑focused policies, and the district has pledged to compare its suspension and expulsion rates against state benchmarks and Blueprint goals.

The equity conversation is deepened by a surge in student homelessness. In July 2025 officials reported a 45 % increase in McKinney‑Vento‑eligible students in Talbot County, mirroring a broader regional rise [15]. Although the county’s overall poverty rate sits at 9.3 %, the spike in homelessness suggests that a growing segment of the student body faces housing instability that can exacerbate academic disengagement and increase the likelihood of disciplinary action. The district’s response includes expanded outreach and coordination with local shelters, yet funding for comprehensive support services remains limited.

Board deliberations have begun to link discipline reform with broader social‑service integration. Proposals under discussion include restorative‑practice training for staff, targeted interventions for at‑risk youth, and a pilot program to embed case managers within schools most affected by homelessness. Until these systemic supports are fully funded and operational, the district risks perpetuating a cycle where students facing housing insecurity are more likely to encounter disciplinary measures that further alienate them from the educational environment. The ongoing review of discipline data and the rising homelessness figures together point to an urgent need for a coordinated equity strategy that addresses both behavior policy and the underlying socioeconomic challenges confronting Talbot’s learners.

## County Overview & Context

**County Overview & Context**

Kent County’s public‑school system serves 1,880 K‑12 students, a modest slice of the county’s 19,289 residents and the 2,311 children aged 5‑17 counted in the 2024 Census. The poverty rate sits at 9.5 % and median household income is $71,635, while 79.4 % of households report broadband access. Racially, the student body is 71.6 % White (1,347), 17.1 % Black or African American (321), and 11.3 % Hispanic/Latino (212); there are no Asian students reported. The enrollment figure trails the school‑age population, indicating that a portion of local youth attend private or out‑of‑district schools.

Academic outcomes under the 2024 MCAP reveal deep gaps. At the elementary level, performance is uneven: Galena Elementary posts 27 % proficiency in Grade 5 ELA, 8 % in Math and 10 % in Science—each well below state averages; H. H. Garnett shows modest gains in ELA (47 % +2.2 pts) but still lags in Math (15 %) and Science (11 %). Rock Hall Elementary is the outlier, meeting or exceeding state expectations in Math (36 % +5.3 pts) and Science (58 % +32.7 pts) while lagging modestly in ELA (36 %). The middle school collapses further, with only 25 % of 8th‑graders proficient in ELA, 5 % in Math and 16.8 % in Science—each well beneath state benchmarks. The high school mirrors this trend: 53.5 % proficiency in ELA (‑6 pts), a stark 6.1 % in Algebra 1 (‑15.3 pts), and 27.7 % in Science (‑16.5 pts). The district’s 2025‑2026 reporting cycle showed little movement from the prior year, underscoring persistent achievement gaps that align with racial and socioeconomic divides highlighted in the Mid‑Shore test‑score analysis [2] and the statewide MCAP release [3].

Governance rests with Superintendent Dr. Mary McComas and a five‑member board (Laura McKenzie, John Queen, Aretha Dorsey, Frank Rhodes, Trish McGee). Recent 2025 coverage illustrates the pressure points the board is confronting. A joint discipline‑trend review launched in October 2025 flagged Kent County’s high suspension counts—143 at the middle school and 133 at the high school for 2023‑2024—prompting a cross‑county equity audit [1]. Simultaneously, a sharp rise in student homelessness (312 students, a 45 % increase) has strained district resources and triggered coordination with local shelters [4]. The Blueprint for Maryland’s Future is reshaping fiscal expectations; Kent County’s participation in the Eastern Shore Blueprint consortium secured a $3 million summer remediation package and a commitment to expand tutoring, as articulated in the March 2025 “bridging gaps” summit [7]. Yet budget constraints remain acute, evidenced by the April 2025 decision to shift Chesapeake College’s new technology‑center costs onto the college rather than county coffers—a move tied to Blueprint financing but reflective of limited local capacity [5]. The confluence of low proficiency, high discipline rates, and growing basic‑needs challenges positions Kent County as a district where the Blueprint’s promised equity and adequacy will be tested in the coming years.

## Top Three Issues on the Education Beat

### Achievement Gaps and Stagnant MCAP Performance  

Kent County’s 2024‑25 MCAP results reveal a district that is barely moving forward while neighboring Mid‑Shore districts experience modest gains. Across the five schools, only H. H. Garnett Elementary managed to exceed the state average in Grade 5 English‑Language Arts, posting a 47.2 % proficiency rate that sits just 2.2 points above the benchmark [2]. The other elementary campuses remain well below expectations: Galena’s 27.0 % ELA and 8.3 % math scores are among the lowest in the county, and Rock Hall’s math proficiency, while above state, still lags at 36.0 % [2]. At the district level, the aggregate proficiency percentages have not shifted since the previous year, mirroring the statewide decline noted in the comprehensive MCAP release [3].  

The data also expose racial disparities that echo the county’s demographic profile. Black or African American students, who comprise 17.1 % of enrollment, are disproportionately represented among those scoring below proficiency, a pattern that aligns with the broader achievement gaps highlighted in the Mid‑Shore mixed‑results story [2]. The persistent underperformance in core subjects has prompted Superintendent Mary McComas to allocate $3 million toward summer remediation programs, yet early indicators suggest the gap remains entrenched [7].  

Compounding the problem is the limited broadband access for nearly one‑fifth of households (79.4 % connectivity) and a poverty rate of 9.5 %, both of which can hinder out‑of‑school learning opportunities. While the Blueprint for Maryland’s Future promises increased tutoring and extended instructional time, the county’s modest gains indicate that implementation is still uneven and that targeted supports have yet to translate into measurable score improvements.

### Rising Student Homelessness and Its Ripple Effects  

The McKinney‑Vento count for Kent County surged by 45 % in the 2024‑25 school year, rising to 312 students who now qualify for homelessness assistance [4]. This jump mirrors a regional trend documented in the July 2025 story on student homelessness, where both Kent and Talbot counties reported the sharpest year‑to‑year increases on the Eastern Shore [4]. Although the county’s overall poverty rate sits below the state average, the concentration of housing instability among school‑aged children is creating a hidden layer of adversity that threatens attendance, engagement, and academic progress.  

Students experiencing homelessness are more likely to miss school, arrive late, or lack the quiet study spaces needed for homework, factors that directly correlate with the stagnant MCAP outcomes observed across the district. The rise in homelessness also places additional strain on school resources, as counselors and social workers must address a growing caseload while maintaining compliance with federal reporting requirements. The district’s response—partnering with local shelters and expanding outreach—has been praised, yet the sheer scale of the need suggests that broader community and state funding will be essential to sustain these efforts.  

Moreover, the homelessness surge underscores the intersection of socioeconomic stressors with the county’s modest broadband penetration. Families without stable housing often lack reliable internet, limiting access to remote learning tools that have become integral since the pandemic. As the district continues to grapple with these intertwined challenges, the pressure to close achievement gaps widens, demanding coordinated policy action that goes beyond classroom instruction.

### Funding Strains, Blueprint Mandates, and Discipline Equity  

Kent County’s education budget is being squeezed from multiple directions as the Blueprint for Maryland’s Future rolls out costly facility upgrades, teacher‑pay raises, and expanded student‑support services. Eastern Shore legislators have repeatedly warned that the mandated “Kirwan” investments could overwhelm local coffers, a concern voiced in February 2025 during a briefing on state education financing [10] and again in January 2025 when county leaders pressed the governor for additional Blueprint aid [12]. The district’s participation in the Blueprint’s summer remediation initiative represents a sizable allocation of already limited resources, leaving little fiscal room for other pressing needs.  

Discipline practices have emerged as another flashpoint for equity and budgetary scrutiny. A recent multi‑county board review, initiated by the Talbot Board of Education in October 2025, will examine suspension and expulsion trends, with a particular focus on racial and socioeconomic disparities [1]. Kent County’s own suspension data—seven suspensions at Galena Elementary and a single suspension at Rock Hall for the 2023‑24 year—may appear modest, but the review aims to ensure that disciplinary actions are applied consistently and do not disproportionately affect minority students. The push for restorative‑justice alternatives, while pedagogically sound, also requires additional staffing and training expenditures that compete with other Blueprint mandates.  

Together, the fiscal pressures of meeting state‑mandated upgrades, the need to sustain robust academic interventions, and the imperative to reform discipline equity create a complex policy landscape. As the county navigates these overlapping demands, beat reporters will need to track how budget decisions translate into classroom realities, whether the Blueprint’s promises of improved outcomes can be realized without exacerbating existing resource gaps, and how the district balances compliance with the broader goal of equitable student success.

As the board negotiates these competing priorities, reporters should monitor how the district’s strategic investments—both in facilities and technology—interact with budgetary constraints and whether they can sustain the modest academic gains achieved in 2025.

## County Overview & Context

**County Overview & Context – Queen Anne’s County (2024‑2025)**  

Queen Anne’s County serves a relatively affluent, largely white student body—6626 K‑12 students (85.6 % White, 8.9 % Hispanic, 4.2 % Black, 1.3 % Asian) out of a total population of 50,316.  The school‑age cohort (5‑17) totals 8,146, with a modest poverty rate of 6.9 % and a median household income of $108,332.  Broadband reaches 86.4 % of households, suggesting that most families have the connectivity needed for the technology‑driven components of the Blueprint for Maryland’s Future.  Nonetheless, the county’s achievement picture is uneven.  While Bayside Elementary posted strong 2024 MCAP results—Grade 5 ELA 59.7 % (+14.7 pts above state) and Math 50.0 % (+19.3 pts)—other schools lag dramatically.  Sudlersville Elementary recorded only 18.3 % proficiency in Grade 5 ELA (‑26.7 pts) and 20.0 % in Math (‑10.7 pts), and Sudlersville Middle posted 5.0 % Math proficiency for Grade 8 (‑3.7 pts) and 16.8 % in Science (‑14.6 pts).  Across the 14 schools, science proficiency is the weakest subject, with only three elementary sites surpassing state averages (Bayside, Matapeake, and Centreville Middle).  The disparity between the high‑performing elementary schools and the struggling middle‑school cohort points to a widening achievement gap as students progress.  The 2024‑2025 MCAP statewide release noted that Queen Anne’s County posted the highest growth among Mid‑Shore districts, with 78 % of students meeting grade‑level standards—a figure that masks the stark intra‑district variation highlighted above [6].

Discipline data for the 2023‑2024 school year further underscores divergent conditions.  Total suspensions are low overall (most elementary sites recorded 1‑2 suspensions), but Centreville Middle registered 18 suspensions, the highest in the district, while Sudlersville Middle reported only two.  The board’s recent decision to launch a county‑wide discipline review, in coordination with neighboring Mid‑Shore districts, reflects growing concern about equity and restorative‑practice implementation [1].  At the same time, the county grappled with broader student‑well‑being issues: a 2025 report documented a surge in student homelessness across the Eastern Shore, including Queen Anne’s, prompting the district to coordinate with local shelters and social‑service agencies [7].

Governance is anchored by an interim superintendent, Dr. Matthew Kibler, who has been steering the district through several high‑profile actions.  In September 2025 the board rejected a charter‑school proposal, citing non‑compliance with Maryland charter law and doubts about alignment with College‑and‑Career‑Ready standards [3].  The board, led by President Shannon Bent and Vice President Alexis Capes, is also navigating Blueprint funding streams.  A joint meeting of Eastern Shore county leaders in September 2025 affirmed a coordinated approach to the Blueprint’s weighted‑funding formulas and to financing the new Chesapeake College technology center, which was approved in April 2025 and aligned with Blueprint objectives [2][8].  These moves illustrate a district that, while benefitting from strong fiscal capacity, must address persistent achievement gaps, discipline equity, and the integration of new Blueprint resources into a stable governance framework.

## Top Three Issues on the Education Beat

### 1. Persistent Achievement Gaps Amid Modest MCAP Gains  

The 2024‑25 Maryland Comprehensive Assessment Program results show Queen Anne’s County edging ahead of many Mid‑Shore peers, with 78 % of students meeting grade‑level standards overall — the highest growth rate in the region — yet the underlying proficiency numbers remain far below state expectations. English proficiency sits at 57 % (down 1.5 pts from the prior year) and math lags at just 31.6 % proficient, an increase of only 2.9 pts [4]. Those headline figures mask stark disparities among schools. Bayside Elementary’s fifth‑grade ELA and math scores sit well above state averages (59.7 % and 50 % respectively), while Grasonville and Church Hill hover at or below parity, with ELA scores in the low‑40s and math only modestly higher than the state [4]. The demographic profile—85.6 % White, 8.9 % Hispanic/Latino, and 4.2 % Black—combined with a relatively low poverty rate (6.9 %) suggests that the achievement gap is less about income and more about access to targeted instructional supports.

County leaders have responded by scaling up tutoring and extending the school year, as outlined in the March 2025 Blueprint‑driven plan that adds 30 minutes of daily remediation and hires an additional 120 counselors across the Mid‑Shore [5]. While the added resources are a step forward, the data indicate that interventions have yet to close the gap for lower‑performing elementary sites, where fifth‑grade science and math still trail state benchmarks. The challenge now is translating system‑wide funding into school‑level capacity, ensuring that the extra tutoring hours reach the classrooms that need them most, and monitoring progress through quarterly assessments mandated by the State Department of Education.

Equity concerns also surface in the discipline data. Although overall suspension numbers are low—Centreville reported only one suspension in 2023‑24—the broader regional review initiated by the Talbot Board of Education underscores the risk that disciplinary practices could disproportionately affect the small but growing Hispanic and Black student populations if not carefully audited [1]. Without a coordinated, data‑driven approach that aligns disciplinary equity with academic remediation, the county may see the same students who are academically behind also experiencing higher exclusion rates, further entrenching the achievement divide.

### 2. Funding Strains and Policy Tensions Around the Blueprint  

Implementing the Blueprint for Maryland’s Future has placed Queen Anne’s County at the intersection of ambitious reform and fiscal reality. County officials, including Commissioner Jack Wilson Jr., have repeatedly warned that the mandated school‑building upgrades, teacher‑pay raises, and expanded support services could overwhelm local budgets, especially given the county’s modest enrollment of 6,626 K‑12 students spread across 14 schools [13]. The recent decision to reject the Queen Anne’s County Classical Charter School application highlights how policy compliance and financial calculations intersect; board members cited insufficient alignment with the Maryland Charter Law and concerns about meeting College and Career Ready Standards as reasons for the 3‑2 vote [2]. While the denial preserves existing resources, it also signals a reluctance to diversify educational options without clear funding guarantees.

The county’s participation in the Chesapeake College budget approval illustrates another funding trade‑off. By shifting the cost of a new technology center onto the college rather than the county, local leaders aimed to protect Blueprint‑related expenditures while still supporting workforce‑training pipelines [8]. However, the move also underscores the delicate balance of allocating limited tax dollars across K‑12 improvements, post‑secondary partnerships, and emergent priorities such as opioid education. The allocation of opioid restitution funds to schools and health‑education programs in August 2025 provides a glimpse of how the county is trying to stretch every revenue source, yet the modest size of those funds means they can only supplement—not replace—core Blueprint investments [3].

Even with a median household income of $108,332, the county’s tax base is spread thin across a relatively low‑density population, limiting the capacity to absorb rising per‑pupil costs. As the Blueprint’s 2026 implementation deadlines approach, county leaders will need to negotiate additional state aid, streamline grant applications, and perhaps reconsider the charter school model as a means to leverage private‑sector capital without compromising fiscal sustainability. The ongoing policy debate will likely center on whether the Blueprint’s long‑term educational benefits justify the near‑term budgetary pressures that are already reshaping board decisions and capital‑project priorities.

### 3. Expanding Student Support Amid Rising Social‑Service Needs  

Beyond academics, Queen Anne’s County is grappling with a widening array of non‑instructional challenges that threaten student success. The regional rise in student homelessness, documented in July 2025, shows shelter referrals doubling across the Eastern Shore, with Queen Anne’s County reporting a noticeable uptick in McKinney‑Vento eligibility [6]. Housing instability correlates strongly with chronic absenteeism and lower academic performance, creating a feedback loop that amplifies existing achievement gaps. County officials have pledged outreach and emergency housing, but the scale of the crisis suggests that school‑based social workers and counselors will need expanded roles and resources to keep displaced students engaged.

At the same time, the state’s Opioid Restitution Fund has been earmarked for school‑based health‑education initiatives, directing limited dollars to opioid awareness programs in Queen Anne’s public schools [3]. While the infusion of targeted health education is welcome, the funding is modest and cannot address the broader mental‑health and substance‑abuse issues surfacing among adolescents. The March 2025 Blueprint‑driven plan to hire 120 additional counselors across the Mid‑Shore reflects an acknowledgement of this gap, but the implementation timeline and staffing pipelines remain uncertain.

Legislative activity adds another layer of complexity. The “No Distracted Learning Act,” introduced in February 2025, seeks to ban personal mobile devices in classrooms across several counties, including Queen Anne’s, as a means to improve focus and safety [7]. While the bill aligns with Blueprint goals of creating a distraction‑free learning environment, it also raises questions about enforcement equity and the potential for punitive discipline to intersect with the already‑sensitive suspension data highlighted in the Talbot Board’s discipline review [1]. As the county navigates these overlapping policy arenas—housing, health, and classroom management—it will need a coordinated strategy that treats student support services as an integral component of educational outcomes, rather than an afterthought.
